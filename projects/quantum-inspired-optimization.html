Overview

This was the work from my third semester coursework on Quantum-Inspired Optimization by Kiran Kumar Hiremath. In this repository, I've attempted to solve multiple combinatorial optimization problems using both classical and quantum-inspired methods.

The project explores the formulation of complex real-world problems into Mixed-Integer Linear Programs (MILP) and Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) forms, then solving them using:

  • Classical Methods: OR-Tools, Pyomo with various solvers
  • Quantum-Inspired Methods: Quantum Annealing (D-Wave), QAOA (Qiskit)

The repository demonstrates the end-to-end pipeline: problem formulation, solver selection, hardware/simulator execution, and performance comparison between classical and quantum approaches.

Bin Packing Problem (BPP)

The Bin Packing Problem is a classic NP-hard optimization problem where the goal is to pack a set of items into a minimum number of bins with given capacity constraints.

Problem Formulation

The BPP is formulated as a Mixed-Integer Linear Program (MILP) with binary decision variables:

  • Objective: Minimize the number of bins used
  • Constraints: Capacity constraints on bins, item-to-bin assignment constraints

Solutions Implemented

Classical Approach

  • Solved using Pyomo with commercial solvers (CPLEX, Gurobi)
  • First-Fit Decreasing (FFD) heuristic for baseline
  • Optimal solution for small problem instances

Quantum Approaches

  • QUBO Formulation: Converted MILP to QUBO for quantum annealing
  • D-Wave Quantum Annealer: Executed on actual quantum hardware
  • QAOA with Qiskit: Variational quantum approach on simulators and QPUs

Key Results

Compared solution quality, execution time, and quantum resource utilization across classical and quantum methods for various problem sizes and constraints.

Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP)

The Vehicle Routing Problem is a combinatorial optimization problem where the goal is to find optimal routes for a fleet of vehicles to serve a set of customers while minimizing total distance/cost.

Problem Formulation

VRP is formulated as an optimization problem with:

  • Objective: Minimize total route distance or operational cost
  • Constraints: Each customer must be served exactly once, vehicle capacity limits, time windows (if applicable)
  • Variants: Capacitated VRP (CVRP), VRP with Time Windows (VRPTW)

Solutions Implemented

Classical Approach

  • OR-Tools: Google's routing optimization library with advanced local search
  • Pyomo: MILP formulation for exact solutions on smaller instances
  • Nearest Neighbor heuristic for baseline solutions

Quantum Approaches

  • QAOA Implementation: Encoding VRP constraints into QUBO
  • Quantum Annealing: Execution on D-Wave with parameter tuning
  • Hybrid Classical-Quantum: Quantum solver for specific subproblems

Key Results

Demonstrated scalability, solution quality trade-offs, and practical considerations when using quantum methods for real-world routing scenarios.

MAX-CUT Problem

The MAX-CUT problem is a canonical NP-hard graph optimization problem: partition the vertices of a graph into two disjoint sets to maximize the number of edges between the two sets.

Why MAX-CUT Matters

  • Fundamental problem in complexity theory and operations research
  • Natural mapping to QUBO, making it ideal for quantum approaches
  • Real applications: circuit partitioning, clustering, resource allocation

Solutions Implemented

Implemented and compared multiple solution approaches demonstrating the quantum advantage potential.

Key Learnings

Problem Formulation

  • Converting real-world problems into MILP and QUBO forms
  • Trade-offs between problem accuracy and solver complexity
  • Penalty terms and constraint encoding for quantum formulations

Classical Optimization

  • Leveraging OR-Tools and Pyomo for rapid prototyping
  • Heuristic vs. exact algorithm selection based on problem size
  • Benchmarking and scalability analysis

Quantum Optimization

  • QAOA ansatz design and parameter optimization
  • D-Wave quantum annealing workflow and embedding constraints
  • Quantum advantage conditions and hybrid classical-quantum strategies

Comparative Analysis

  • When quantum methods are practical vs. theoretical
  • NISQ-era limitations and workarounds
  • Future roadmap as quantum hardware matures

Conclusion

This project provided hands-on experience in formulating combinatorial optimization problems, solving them with state-of-the-art classical and quantum methods, and understanding the current practical landscape of quantum optimization. While quantum advantage remains elusive for small instances, the experience of working with real quantum hardware and understanding its constraints is invaluable for developing future quantum-classical hybrid algorithms.
