Overview

This was the work from my third semester coursework on Quantum-Inspired Optimization by Kiran Kumar Hiremath. In this repository, I've attempted to solve multiple combinatorial optimization problems using both classical and quantum-inspired methods.

The project explores the formulation of complex real-world problems into Mixed-Integer Linear Programs (MILP) and Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) forms, then solving them using:

  • Classical Methods: OR-Tools, Pyomo with various solvers
  • Quantum-Inspired Methods: Quantum Annealing (D-Wave), QAOA (Qiskit)

The repository demonstrates the end-to-end pipeline: problem formulation, solver selection, hardware/simulator execution, and performance comparison between classical and quantum approaches.

Key Learnings

Problem Formulation

  • Converting real-world problems into MILP and QUBO forms
  • Trade-offs between problem accuracy and solver complexity
  • Penalty terms and constraint encoding for quantum formulations

Classical Optimization

  • Leveraging OR-Tools and Pyomo for rapid prototyping
  • Heuristic vs. exact algorithm selection based on problem size
  • Benchmarking and scalability analysis

Quantum Optimization

  • QAOA ansatz design and parameter optimization
  • D-Wave quantum annealing workflow and embedding constraints
  • Quantum advantage conditions and hybrid classical-quantum strategies

Comparative Analysis

  • When quantum methods are practical vs. theoretical
  • NISQ-era limitations and workarounds
  • Future roadmap as quantum hardware matures

Technical Stack

Quantum Computing

  • Qiskit: QAOA implementation and circuit manipulation
  • D-Wave Ocean SDK: Quantum annealing via D-Wave systems
  • Cirq: Quantum circuit abstractions (where applicable)

Classical Optimization

  • OR-Tools: Vehicle routing and constraint-based optimization
  • Pyomo: MILP modeling and solver integration
  • NumPy/SciPy: Numerical and optimization utilities

Analysis & Visualization

  • Pandas/Matplotlib: Results analysis and plotting
  • NetworkX: Graph representations for optimization problems

Conclusion

This project provided hands-on experience in formulating combinatorial optimization problems, solving them with state-of-the-art classical and quantum methods, and understanding the current practical landscape of quantum optimization. While quantum advantage remains elusive for small instances, the experience of working with real quantum hardware and understanding its constraints is invaluable for developing future quantum-classical hybrid algorithms.
